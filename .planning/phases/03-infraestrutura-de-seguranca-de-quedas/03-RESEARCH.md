## Summary

Phase 3 produces one protocol — P-07 Quedas e Síndrome Pós-Queda — that serves a dual role analogous to P-08 and P-04 in Phase 2: it is simultaneously a standalone clinical protocol and a cross-protocol safety infrastructure reference. The two mandatory deliverables within P-07 are (1) the balance intervention hierarchy with objective entry criteria for each of its four levels (static, dynamic, dual-task, perturbation training), and (2) the session-level fall risk protocol formatted as a reusable template that all subsequent protocols in Phases 4–8 will embed or cite.

The balance training hierarchy is strongly supported by current evidence. The World Falls Guidelines 2022 (WFG 2022, PubMed 36178003) provide Grade 1A (strong, high-quality evidence) for balance and functional exercise programmes at ≥3×/week over ≥12 weeks. A 2025 meta-analysis confirms dual-task exercise significantly improves dynamic balance and reduces fall frequency. Perturbation-based balance training (PBT) has a rapidly maturing evidence base: a 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis showed PBT reduces fall rates by 23% versus controls, outperforming traditional balance training, and a feasibility pilot (PMC10587291) confirmed PBT is achievable in pre-frail and frail geriatric patients with appropriate harness support and pre-testing. The hierarchy must specify objective TUG-and-SPPB-anchored entry criteria for each level so any protocol can unambiguously determine which tier a patient begins on.

P-07 must also include rigorous kinesiophobia and fear-of-falling screening because post-fall syndrome is as much a psychological as a physical condition. The FES-I-BR (Camargos et al. 2010, PubMed 20730369) is the validated Brazilian Portuguese fear-of-falling measure with established cutoffs (≥23 sporadic falls; ≥31 recurrent falls). The TSK-11-BR (Salvador et al. 2020, Braz J Phys Ther) is the validated Brazilian Portuguese kinesiophobia instrument with adequate psychometric properties. Both must appear as mandatory screening tools in P-07's assessment section.

**Primary recommendation:** Build P-07 as a 15-section protocol following the established template, with two clearly labeled modifier-level sections: (A) the balance intervention hierarchy table as a reusable reference for all neuromotor and frailty protocols (Phases 4–8), and (B) the session-level fall risk protocol formatted as an extractable template block. The session-level protocol must specify four checklist elements: pre-session fatigue VAS, orthostatic BP check, TUG-anchored gait belt criteria, and supervised exit protocol.

|-------|------|--------------------|--------------------|----------------|

### Pattern 2: Session-Level Fall Risk Protocol — Reusable Template Block

**What:** A 4-element pre/intra/post-session protocol that defines the minimum safety checks before and during every balance/rehabilitation session with fall-risk patients. This block is formatted for embedding verbatim or by citation in all Phase 4–8 protocols.

**Format:**

```markdown
> **INFRAESTRUTURA DE SEGURANÇA — PROTOCOLO DE RISCO DE QUEDA POR SESSÃO**
> Este protocolo é o template de segurança intra-sessão para todos os protocolos
> desta série. Qualquer protocolo que atenda pacientes com risco de queda (TUG > 13,5
> seg OU FES-I ≥ 23 OU ≥ 1 queda no último ano) deve aplicar este protocolo ou
> citar: *"Aplicar Protocolo de Risco de Queda por Sessão conforme P-07, Seção 5.X."*

### Protocolo de Risco de Queda por Sessão (P-07)

**Elemento 1 — Verificação de Fadiga (VAS Pré-Sessão)**
Antes de iniciar qualquer exercício de equilíbrio:
- Aplicar VAS de fadiga (0–10 cm): "Como está seu nível de cansaço agora?"
- VAS ≤ 4: prosseguir com protocolo completo
- VAS 5–7: reduzir volume em 30%; eliminar exercícios de perturbação; monitorar a cada 10 min
- VAS ≥ 8: adiar sessão ou limitar a mobilização funcional leve (marcha curta com cinto); comunicar equipe médica

**Elemento 2 — Verificação de PA Ortostática Pré-Sessão**
Indicação: todo paciente em uso de anti-hipertensivos, diuréticos, antidepressivos, ou com histórico de tontura ao levantar.
- Protocolo: PA deitado (5 min repouso) → PA em pé (1 min e 3 min após levantar)
- Normal: queda sistólica < 20 mmHg OU diastólica < 10 mmHg
- Hipotensão ortostática: queda sistólica ≥ 20 mmHg OU diastólica ≥ 10 mmHg
- Se hipotensão ortostática presente: adiar exercícios de balanço ereto; apenas exercícios sentados; comunicar clínico

**Elemento 3 — Critérios de Cinto Pélvico por Pontuação TUG**
| TUG | Ação de Segurança |
|-----|-------------------|
| ≤ 10 seg | Cinto pélvico opcional; supervisão contato-guarda se < 3 sessões de adaptação |
| 10,1–13,5 seg | Cinto pélvico recomendado para todos os exercícios em pé |
| 13,6–20 seg | Cinto pélvico obrigatório; 1 terapeuta ao lado para todos os exercícios em pé |
| > 20 seg | Cinto pélvico obrigatório + suporte adicional (paralelas ou assento de apoio imediato) |

*Nota: TUG medido na avaliação inicial; reavaliado a cada 4 semanas ou após evento adverso.*

**Elemento 4 — Protocolo de Saída Supervisionada**
Ao final de cada sessão:
1. 5 minutos de atividade de resfriamento em posição sentada
2. Verificação de tontura antes de levantar ("Está tonto?")
3. Levantar assistido com cinto pélvico se TUG > 13,5 seg na avaliação
4. Aguardar 2 min em pé antes de liberar para deambulação independente
5. Não liberar paciente que relate tontura, instabilidade ou NRS > 4 pós-exercício
6. Registrar no prontuário: VAS fadiga pré/pós, PA se verificada, qualquer intercorrência
```

### Pattern 3: Kinesiophobia Screening Integration (TSK-11-BR + FES-I-BR)

**What:** Both instruments serve different but complementary purposes. FES-I-BR measures concern/fear related to specific activities (behavioral). TSK-11-BR measures the belief that movement will cause reinjury (cognitive-affective). Both must be screened at intake.

**Decision logic:**

```markdown
### Triagem de Cinesiofobia e Medo de Cair (Avaliação Inicial P-07)

Aplicar ambos na avaliação inicial:

| Instrumento | Pontuação | Interpretação | Ação Clínica |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| FES-I-BR | 16–22 | Baixo medo de cair | Programa de equilíbrio padrão |
| FES-I-BR | 23–30 | Medo de cair — histórico esporádico | Componente psicoeducacional + progressão gradual |
| FES-I-BR | ≥ 31 | Alto medo de cair — histórico recorrente | Escalonamento: psicólogo/geriatra + programa estruturado de dessensibilização gradual ao movimento |

| TSK-11-BR | > 37 | Cinesiofobia clinicamente significativa | Intervenção psicoeducacional prioritária antes de progredir a exercícios de equilíbrio desafiadores |
```

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Hierarquia sem critérios objetivos de entrada:** Descrever níveis de treino de equilíbrio sem TUG/SPPB/BBS como gatilhos de entrada — deixa o clínico sem âncora funcional. Cada nível DEVE ter critérios objetivos mensuráveis.
- **Treinamento de perturbação sem arnês:** A literatura é clara — arnês de segurança é obrigatório (PMC10587291). Prescrever PBT sem especificar o arnês como requisito de equipamento é erro de segurança.
- **Protocolo de sessão como recomendação genérica:** Escrever "monitorar paciente durante a sessão" sem elementos operacionais (VAS, PA, TUG-cinto) não atende ao padrão de template reutilizável exigido pelo sucesso da fase.
- **FES-I-BR sem cutoffs:** Aplicar FES-I-BR mas não interpretar com os cutoffs validados (≥23/≥31) anula o valor da triagem.
- **Classificar síndrome pós-queda como "medo de cair":** Post-fall syndrome é a tríade medo + restrição de atividade + perda funcional progressiva — não é apenas medo. O protocolo deve abordar os três componentes.
- **Omitir TSK-11-BR:** Usar apenas FES-I-BR sem TSK-11-BR perde a dimensão cognitivo-afetiva da cinesiofobia — especialmente relevante em pacientes que evitam certos movimentos mesmo sem queda prévia.
- **Progressão por tempo, não por função:** Avançar de nível na hierarquia após "4 semanas" sem verificar os critérios de saída (TUG, BBS, FES-I). Critérios funcionais objetivos são mandatórios conforme DOSE-04.

## Don't Hand-Roll

| Problem | Don't Build | Use Instead | Why |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Fear of falling measurement | Ad hoc questionnaire or VAS para medo | FES-I-BR (Camargos 2010, PubMed 20730369) | Cronbach α = 0.93; ICC = 0.91; validated cutoffs for sporadic (≥23) and recurrent (≥31) falls; gold standard in falls research |
| Kinesiophobia screening | Asking "tem medo de se machucar?" | TSK-11-BR (Salvador 2020, PMC7990729) | Validated 11-item Brazilian Portuguese version; captures movement-specific fear beyond general fear of falling; ICC = 0.85 |
| Balance severity classification | Custom balance "levels" without anchor | BBS-BR (Miyamoto 2004, PubMed 15334208) | 14-task standardized tool; fall risk cutoff < 45; Brazilian validation; MCIDs published |
| Balance training hierarchy | Arbitrary tier system | Evidence-based 4-level hierarchy (static → dynamic → dual-task → perturbation) from WFG 2022 + meta-analyses | Each tier has distinct mechanisms and evidence base; custom hierarchies lack cross-study validity |
| Fall risk stratification (global) | Custom clinic scoring | WFG 2022 3-tier risk stratification (low/intermediate/high) + STEADI algorithm | Internationally validated; aligns primary care and PT assessment; avoids parallel incompatible systems |
| Perturbation training safety | Trial-and-error approach | Arnês de segurança + pré-teste sem perturbações (1–3 min) — derived from PMC10587291 feasibility study | Reduces dropout from 31% to 12%; no adverse events recorded when properly applied |
| Kinesiophobia treatment escalation | "Refer to psychology" without criteria | TSK-11-BR > 37 + FES-I-BR ≥ 31 = escalation threshold (derived from psychometric properties and clinical consensus) | Operationalizes referral — prevents under- and over-referral |

**Key insight:** Falls prevention involves both physical (balance, strength) and psychological (fear, kinesiophobia) dimensions that cannot be adequately addressed with custom tools. Validated instruments provide cutoffs, MCIDs, and normative data that enable objective treatment decisions and outcome tracking.

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Treating Post-Fall Syndrome as Synonymous with Fear of Falling
**What goes wrong:** Describing "síndrome pós-queda" as equivalent to "medo de cair" — omitting the functional restriction and progressive deconditioning components.
**Why it happens:** Murphy & Isaacs 1982 (the original description) focused heavily on fear, and that label stuck.
**How to avoid:** P-07 must define the full triad: (1) medo de cair (FES-I-BR ≥ 23), (2) restrição de atividade (avoidance behavior), AND (3) perda funcional progressiva (TUG deterioration, SPPB decline). Treatment must address all three.
**Warning signs:** A protocol section on post-fall syndrome that only addresses fear without exercise deconditioning and activity progression.

### Pitfall 2: Balance Hierarchy Without Objective Entry Criteria
**What goes wrong:** Describing "static balance training," "dynamic balance training," "dual-task training," and "perturbation training" as sequential levels but not specifying TUG/SPPB/BBS values that determine which level a patient enters.
**Why it happens:** Authors assume clinicians will use clinical judgment. Clinical judgment without anchors leads to inconsistency across therapists.
**How to avoid:** Each level in the hierarchy table MUST have a "Critério de Entrada" column with specific TUG, SPPB, and BBS values. See Pattern 1 above.
**Warning signs:** Hierarchy table with rows for levels but no measurable entry criteria.

### Pitfall 3: Omitting Perturbation Training as a Valid Option for Frail Patients
**What goes wrong:** Restricting perturbation training to "robust" or "community-dwelling" patients, excluding frail patients who could benefit.
**Why it happens:** Perturbation training sounds dangerous for fragile patients.
**How to avoid:** PMC10587291 confirmed 61% of participants in the perturbation treadmill pilot were frail (Fried criteria), and 35% were pre-frail — all completed training with safety harness and pre-test protocol. P-07 should include perturbation training for appropriate frail patients with the correct safeguards, not exclude categorically.
**Warning signs:** A table that marks "Nível 4 — Perturbação" as available only for "Robusto" patients without clinical evidence for the restriction.

### Pitfall 4: Session-Level Protocol as a Bullet List, Not a Template
**What goes wrong:** Listing "verify BP, check fatigue, use gait belt" as informal bullets without specifying thresholds, decision trees, and escalation criteria.
**Why it happens:** Session safety feels like common sense; detailed operationalization seems excessive.
**How to avoid:** The Success Criteria for this phase explicitly require a "reusable section template" — this means it must be formatted with specific VAS thresholds, BP numbers, TUG-gait belt criteria table, and exit protocol steps that another protocol can copy verbatim. See Pattern 2 above.
**Warning signs:** Safety elements described in narrative prose rather than decision-table format.

### Pitfall 5: FES-I-BR Cutoffs Confused Between Studies
**What goes wrong:** Citing cutoff of ≥ 23 from one study and ≥ 24 from another without specifying the validation source.
**Why it happens:** Multiple papers report slightly different cutoffs for FES-I-BR depending on the population studied.
**How to avoid:** Use Camargos et al. 2010 (PubMed 20730369, Rev Bras Fisioter) as the canonical validation source. Cutoffs: ≥ 23 = sporadic fall history; ≥ 31 = recurrent fall history. Note these were derived in a community-dwelling Brazilian sample (n=163, mean age 73.4 years) — the target population for this protocol series.
**Warning signs:** FES-I-BR cutoff cited without the Camargos 2010 reference.

### Pitfall 6: Orthostatic Hypotension Check Not Standardized
**What goes wrong:** Instructing "verify blood pressure before exercise" without specifying the standardized protocol (supine 5 min → stand 1 min → stand 3 min) and the diagnostic threshold (≥ 20 mmHg systolic drop OR ≥ 10 mmHg diastolic drop).
**Why it happens:** Orthostatic BP checking is assumed to be familiar to all therapists.
**How to avoid:** The session-level template MUST specify the exact protocol. The diagnostic criterion is from international consensus (Journal of Internal Medicine 2021, PMC10024337): systolic drop ≥ 20 mmHg or diastolic drop ≥ 10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing.
**Warning signs:** BP check mentioned without supine baseline and standing measurement timing.

### Pitfall 7: Cross-Protocol Modifier Label Missing
**What goes wrong:** The balance hierarchy table appears as a section within P-07 without the mandatory "INFRAESTRUTURA DE SEGURANÇA — REFERÊNCIA CRUZADA" label, so downstream protocols have no explicit signal that this is the authoritative cross-protocol reference.
**Why it happens:** The modifier label pattern from Phase 2 may seem redundant when the content is already in P-07.
**How to avoid:** The blockquote callout label (as in P-08 Section 6.3 and P-04 Section 5.2) is mandatory. The label must name which protocols should cite P-07 and specify the exact citation format: "Progressão de equilíbrio conforme P-07 — Tabela de Hierarquia de Intervenção de Equilíbrio (Seção X.X)."
**Warning signs:** Balance hierarchy table without a bold callout box identifying it as a cross-protocol reference.

## Open Questions

1. **TSK-11-BR Validated Cutoff Specifically for Geriatric Falls Population**
   - What we know: TSK-11-BR (Salvador 2020, PMC7990729) was validated in a fibromyalgia population (mean age 45.5 years, 98.5% female) — not in geriatric falls patients.
   - What's unclear: Whether the 11–44 scale's clinically meaningful threshold (often cited as > 37) is valid for community-dwelling elders with fall history.
   - Recommendation: Use > 37 as the escalation threshold with annotation: "Ponto de corte derivado de populações com dor crônica; verificar literatura específica de quedas em idosos antes da publicação definitiva." Research Siqueira FB et al. 2007 (Acta Ortop Bras 15:19-24) for original Brazilian TSK-13 validation — may provide additional context.

2. **BBS-BR MCID in Geriatric Falls Population**
   - What we know: BBS Brazilian version validated by Miyamoto et al. 2004 (PubMed 15334208). MCID of 3.3–6.5 points cited from Donoghue C et al. 2009 (Arch Phys Med Rehabil) — derived in elderly population.
   - What's unclear: Whether the Donoghue MCID applies specifically to the Brazilian geriatric population and the BBS-BR version.
   - Recommendation: Use 3.3 points as conservative MCID; cite Donoghue 2009; annotate that Brazilian population-specific MCID data is not confirmed.

3. **TSK-13 (13-item) vs TSK-11 (11-item) — Which Version for P-07?**
   - What we know: Both versions have Portuguese/Brazilian adaptations. The TSK-13 has the Siqueira 2007 Brazilian validation. The TSK-11 has the Salvador 2020 Brazilian validation (specifically "Brazilian-Portuguese version"). The TSK-11 is psychometrically cleaner (removed 6 items with poor fit).
   - What's unclear: Which version is more commonly used in Brazilian geriatric falls research.
   - Recommendation: Use TSK-11-BR (Salvador 2020) as the primary version — shorter, cleaner psychometrics, peer-reviewed validation. Note TSK-13 as historical alternative.

4. **Session Fall Risk Protocol — VAS Fatigue Threshold Evidence Base**
   - What we know: VAS fatigue (0–10 cm) is widely used in rehabilitation. The thresholds proposed (≤4/5–7/≥8) are clinical consensus from rehabilitation practice, not derived from a specific RCT.
   - What's unclear: Whether a peer-reviewed source explicitly recommends VAS fatigue cutoffs for session modification in geriatric fall prevention specifically.
   - Recommendation: Use the proposed thresholds with annotation: "Baseado em consenso clínico de prática estabelecida em reabilitação geriátrica — verificar literatura específica de VAS fadiga pré-exercício em idosos com risco de queda antes da publicação definitiva."

5. **Perturbation Training in Brazilian Geriatric Settings — Equipment Access**
   - What we know: The strongest evidence for perturbation training involves treadmill-based systems with overhead harness — equipment that may not be available in all Brazilian geriatric rehabilitation centres.
   - What's unclear: Whether the P-07 hierarchy should specify non-treadmill perturbation alternatives (manual platform, foam surfaces, therapist-applied perturbations) as accessible options.
   - Recommendation: Include both treadmill-PBT (Nível 4A — gold standard) and manual-perturbation alternatives (Nível 4B — therapist-applied, foam surface, gym ball) with distinct safety protocols. Annotate that treadmill-PBT has stronger evidence; manual alternatives are used when equipment is unavailable.
