# Feature Landscape: Evidence-Based Geriatric Rehabilitation Protocols

**Domain:** Clinical Protocol Documentation — Geriatric Physical Therapy
**Researched:** 2026-03-12
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — Based on training knowledge (cutoff August 2025) from AGS, WHO, IASP, APTA, Cochrane, JOSPT, PTJ, and systematic review literature. Web access was unavailable for real-time verification; claims reflect established international consensus as of knowledge cutoff.

---

## Table Stakes

Features that every protocol must have for clinical validity. Missing any of these renders a protocol clinically incomplete or unsafe for application in a specialized rehabilitation setting.

| Feature | Why Expected | Complexity | Dependencies |
|---------|--------------|------------|--------------|
| **Diagnostic criteria / Inclusion criteria** | Clinicians must know exactly which patients qualify for the protocol; prevents misapplication to wrong population | Low | None — this is the entry gate |
| **Contraindications and precautions** | Patient safety baseline; absence is a liability and ethical failure | Low-Medium | Requires diagnosis, comorbidity staging, and red flag list |
| **Baseline functional assessments (TUG, SPPB, Manual Dynamometry)** | Standardized starting point for progression decisions and outcome measurement; required by AGS/WHO functional assessment standards | Medium | Must precede Phase 1 interventions |
| **Clinical phase structure (Acute/Subacute/Functional or condition-appropriate equivalent)** | Temporal organization of interventions; enables safe progression and prevents premature loading | Medium | Depends on baseline assessments to assign starting phase |
| **Prescriptive exercise parameters (sets, reps, frequency, intensity)** | Without dosage, a protocol is a list of ideas, not a clinical instruction; essential for reproducibility and evidence alignment | Medium | Depends on phase structure and evidence-based dosage from RCTs |
| **Acceptable parameter ranges for clinical adaptation** | Geriatric patients have high intra-population variability; rigid single values are clinically unrealistic and contraindicated by "Start low, go slow" principle | Low-Medium | Depends on prescriptive parameters being defined first |
| **Progression criteria (within-phase and phase-advancement)** | Tells clinician when to advance or hold; absence leads to arbitrary decisions that deviate from evidence | Medium | Depends on baseline assessments and functional benchmarks |
| **Intervention classification by modality** | Therapeutic Modality, Manual Therapy, Kinesiotherapy categories enable systematic cross-referencing and curriculum alignment | Low | None — structural/organizational |
| **Primary outcome indicators per phase** | Defines what "improvement" looks like; enables audit and clinical decision-making | Medium | Depends on baseline assessments; feeds re-evaluation schedule |
| **Re-evaluation schedule / checkpoints** | Geriatric patients can deteriorate or plateau; scheduled reassessment is patient safety infrastructure | Low | Depends on outcome indicators and phase structure |
| **Red flags and stopping rules** | Clinical conditions requiring immediate protocol suspension or escalation to acute care | Low-Medium | Requires contraindications and outcome indicator baselines |
| **Evidence level classification per intervention** | Differentiates Level A (RCT/meta-analysis) from Level B/C; intellectual honesty and clinical trust | Medium | Requires literature sourcing per intervention |
| **Guideline alignment declaration** | Explicit statement of alignment to IASP, AGS, WHO, or equivalent; establishes epistemic authority | Low | Requires evidence level classification |
| **"Start low, go slow, but reach the goal" dosage philosophy embedded in protocol** | Core geriatric prescriptive principle; prevents under- and over-dosing in a fragile population | Low | Embedded across parameter ranges and progression criteria |

## Differentiators

Features that elevate a protocol from adequate to excellent — not universally required, but strongly value-adding for clinical application quality, safety, training value, and cross-protocol coherence.

| Feature | Value Proposition | Complexity | Dependencies |
|---------|-------------------|------------|--------------|
| **Clinical decision tree / flowchart** | Enables rapid at-a-glance decisions during patient sessions; converts linear text into navigable clinical logic | High | Requires phase structure and progression criteria to be finalized |
| **Structured red flag table with escalation pathways** | Goes beyond listing contraindications — maps specific warning signs to specific responses; critical for junior clinicians | Medium | Depends on contraindications and red flags being defined |
| **Pain monitoring integration (NRS/VAS thresholds per phase)** | Pain is the primary limiting factor in geriatric rehabilitation; explicit thresholds align with IASP pain management principles | Medium | Depends on phase structure; references IASP guidelines |
| **Functional benchmark targets at phase transitions** | "Patient must achieve TUG ≤ 14s to advance to Phase 2" — transforms progression from subjective to objective | Medium | Depends on TUG, SPPB, dynamometry baselines |
| **Cross-protocol comorbidity flags** | Notes how a condition (e.g., Parkinson's) modifies protocol execution for a patient also treated under another protocol (e.g., Hip Fracture) | High | Requires multiple protocols to exist; sequential development means this matures late |
| **Patient education summary (key instructions for home)** | Empowers patient adherence between sessions; aligns with WHO self-management frameworks | Medium | Depends on exercise parameters; requires plain-language translation of clinical content |
| **Home exercise program (HEP) template** | Bridges clinic-to-home gap; dramatically improves outcomes in geriatric populations with limited session frequency | Medium | Depends on exercise parameters and phase structure |
| **Caregiver / family guidance section** | High-value for dementia, stroke, Parkinson's, and frailty protocols where caregiver co-participation is evidence-based | Medium | Relevant only to specific comorbidities (cognitive, neurological, high-dependency) |
| **Adherence and motivation strategies specific to geriatric population** | Dropout is the primary threat to protocol effectiveness; geriatric-specific motivational strategies differ from general population | Medium | None structurally; draws on behavioral and geriatric psychology literature |
| **Differential diagnosis considerations** | Helps clinician confirm the protocol is the right one (e.g., distinguishing osteoarthritis from other knee pathologies) | Medium | Should precede inclusion criteria; adds depth for academic/training use |
| **Specific equipment requirements and substitutions** | Informs resource planning; substitutions enable use in lower-resource settings | Low | Depends on intervention classification |
| **Dosage rationale annotations** | Brief in-protocol notes citing why a specific dosage was chosen (e.g., "3×10 based on Fragala et al. 2019 meta-analysis") | High | Requires evidence sourcing per parameter; high documentation overhead |
| **Short-form quick reference card** | 1-page distillation for experienced clinicians; full protocol is for training and audit, quick card is for daily use | Medium | Depends on full protocol being finalized first |
| **Intra-session structure template (warm-up, main, cool-down)** | Converts parameter list into session-ready structure; saves clinical planning time | Medium | Depends on exercise parameters and phase structure |

## Anti-Features

Features to deliberately exclude from these protocols. Including them would compromise clinical validity, scope, or usability.

| Anti-Feature | Why Avoid | What to Do Instead |
|--------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Proprietary or trademarked technique names without scientific validation** | Creates dependency on brand names rather than mechanisms; e.g., "Brand X vibration therapy" instead of "whole-body vibration at 25-30Hz" | Describe the mechanism and parameters from validated studies |
| **Unvalidated or fringe modalities (e.g., homeopathy, low-quality electrotherapy)** | Violates Level A evidence standard; damages protocol credibility; harms patients through opportunity cost | Exclude entirely; if evidence is Level C or lower, mark as such and reserve for adjunct/optional sections |
| **Acute hospital care phases** | Out of scope per PROJECT.md; different population state, different clinical logic, different legal context | Begin each protocol at the post-acute/rehabilitation phase entry point |
| **Surgical technique descriptions** | Out of scope; creates confusion between surgical and rehabilitation teams; clinician liability risk | Reference surgical approach only as a contextual note that informs precautions (e.g., "posterior approach ATQ — hip precautions apply") |
| **Pharmaceutical dosage recommendations** | Outside physical therapy scope of practice; creates medico-legal exposure | Reference pharmacological management as "per prescribing physician" where clinically relevant (e.g., pain management in osteoarthritis) |
| **Pediatric or adult non-geriatric content** | Dilutes population specificity; geriatric physiology is distinct (sarcopenia, polypharmacy, cognitive load, fall risk) | Every protocol must explicitly state geriatric population parameters |
| **Marketing language or quality-of-care claims without evidence** | Undermines scientific credibility; out of scope per PROJECT.md | Use only clinically precise, evidence-referenced language |
| **Generic/universal exercise programs not tailored to condition** | A "general strengthening program" is not a protocol; fails the clinical validity standard | Every exercise selection must be condition-specific and phase-appropriate |
| **Single rigid dosage without ranges** | Clinically dangerous in a population with high variability; ignores "Start low, go slow" principle | All dosages must include recommended value PLUS acceptable adaptation range |
| **Outcome measures without normative reference values** | "TUG score = 18s" means nothing without knowing expected range for age/condition | Include age-stratified normative values for all functional outcome tools used |
| **Cross-population evidence without geriatric-specific notes** | Study conducted in 35-year-olds cannot be applied unchanged to 78-year-olds; adaptation must be explicit | When using non-geriatric RCTs as the best available evidence, flag this with a clinical adaptation note |

## MVP Recommendation

**For the first protocol (Osteoarthritis) and each subsequent protocol, prioritize:**

1. Diagnostic/inclusion criteria + contraindications + red flags
2. Baseline assessments (TUG, SPPB, Manual Dynamometry) with normative values
3. Clinical phase structure (3 phases recommended for OA as the template)
4. Prescriptive exercise parameters with acceptable ranges, evidence-classified
5. Progression criteria (objective, functional benchmark-based)
6. Intervention classification (Therapeutic Modality / Manual Therapy / Kinesiotherapy)
7. Primary outcome indicators per phase + re-evaluation schedule
8. Guideline alignment declaration (IASP, AGS, WHO where applicable)
9. Pain monitoring with NRS thresholds per phase [differentiator worth including from Phase 1 — IASP alignment]
10. Structured red flag table with escalation pathways [high safety value, medium effort]

**Defer to later protocols or later versions:**
- Cross-protocol comorbidity flags: cannot be built until 3+ protocols exist
- Quick reference cards: build after template is stable across 2-3 protocols
- Dosage rationale annotations: high overhead; add selectively for contested or non-obvious parameters
- Caregiver guidance: prioritize for Parkinson's (Protocol 6), Dementia (Protocol 18), Stroke (Protocol 3) — not needed for OA template

## Clinical Validity Threshold

A protocol passes the clinical validity threshold when it answers all of the following questions without ambiguity:

1. Who is this protocol for? (Inclusion / exclusion)
2. When should we stop? (Contraindications, red flags, stopping rules)
3. Where do we start? (Baseline assessments, phase assignment)
4. What do we do? (Intervention classification and exercise selection)
5. How much? (Prescriptive parameters with ranges)
6. How fast? (Progression criteria, re-evaluation schedule)
7. How do we know it's working? (Outcome indicators, functional benchmarks)
8. Why these choices? (Evidence level, guideline alignment)

Any protocol that cannot answer all eight questions is incomplete regardless of document length.
